Why Used EV Prices Just Fell Off a Cliff

Battery anxiety, fast depreciation, and what it means for buyers this year.

By Priya Nadan · July 19, 2026 · 5 min read

A three-year-old electric sedan that sold new for fifty thousand dollars is now changing hands for barely half that. Dealers describe used EV lots that used to move inventory in weeks now sitting for months, and the reasons go well beyond the usual story about new-model price cuts.

The battery question nobody can answer confidently

Every used EV carries an invisible variable that used gas cars never had to deal with: how much battery capacity is actually left, and how much it would cost to replace it if it fails outside warranty. Independent battery health reports exist, but they're inconsistent across manufacturers and rarely trusted the way a mechanic's inspection of an engine is trusted.

Depreciation curves that don't behave like gas cars

Because EV technology has moved quickly — range, charging speed, software — a three-year-old EV can feel meaningfully more dated than a three-year-old gas car ever did. Buyers factor that obsolescence risk into what they're willing to pay, and sellers are discovering the discount required is steeper than anyone forecasted two years ago.

None of this means EVs were a bad purchase. It means the used market hasn't yet figured out how to price them fairly, and until standardized battery health reporting becomes the norm, buyers willing to do their own diligence stand to get real value at today's prices.
