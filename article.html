The Bugatti Chiron Is the Last of Its Kind — And It Knows It

As electric hypercars take over, we drive the final gasoline-powered flagship from Molsheim and ask what gets lost in the transition.

By Marco Reyes · July 18, 2026 · 9 min read

There is a specific sound a W16 engine makes at idle that no electric motor will ever reproduce, and standing next to one for the first time is the closest thing the automotive world has to a religious experience. The Chiron does not so much start as it detonates quietly into life, eight cylinders on each side settling into a rhythm that feels less like machinery and more like breathing.

Bugatti built its reputation on excess that somehow reads as restraint. The Chiron's numbers are almost comedic on paper — over 1,500 horsepower, a top speed once electronically capped at 261 miles per hour, a price tag that starts around three million dollars before a single option is ticked. And yet in person, the car is quiet in a way its numbers do not suggest, finished in a way that makes hypercars from a decade earlier look almost unfinished by comparison.

The end of an era, on purpose

What makes this particular car different is timing. Bugatti has confirmed the combustion-only Chiron platform is done, its successor arriving with a hybridized V16 that keeps some of the drama but changes the fundamental physics of what's under the hood. That makes every remaining Chiron on a dealer lot a kind of rolling artifact, the last of a lineage that traces back through the Veyron to a philosophy of building cars with no real compromise.

"We didn't design it to be the last. We designed it to be the best. It just happens to also be the last."

That sentiment, from someone close to the development program, captures something true about how these cars get made. Nobody sets out to build a monument. They set out to solve an engineering problem — how do you put this much power on the road without it becoming unusable — and the monument happens as a side effect.

What actually gets lost

Electric hypercars are, by most objective measures, already faster. The acceleration numbers from a handful of upcoming EV hypercars make the Chiron look almost leisurely off the line. But acceleration was never really the point of a car like this. The point was theater — the sound building through the rev range, the mechanical sympathy of watching sixteen cylinders work in concert, the sense that you are commanding something alive rather than programming something efficient.

None of that is a knock against electric performance cars, which are extraordinary in their own right and solve different problems entirely. It's simply an acknowledgment that something specific and hard to quantify disappears when the engine goes away, and the Chiron may be remembered less for its top speed than for being one of the last cars where that theater was still the whole point.

What it's actually like to drive

On a mountain road outside Molsheim, the Chiron feels almost absurdly composed for something this powerful. The all-wheel-drive system and active aerodynamics do a remarkable job of hiding the car's weight, and it's genuinely usable at low speeds in a way that its numbers don't suggest. It is, somewhat improbably, comfortable — a car you could drive to dinner rather than one that demands a closed circuit and a helmet.

That usability may end up being the Chiron's real legacy: proof that a car can be simultaneously the most extreme thing on the road and one of the easiest to actually live with. Whatever comes next from Bugatti will have to reckon with that combination, hybrid powertrain or not.
