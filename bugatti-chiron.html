The Chiron Era Is Over. Its Hybrid V16 Successor Doesn't Share a Single Part With It.

After eight years and 500 cars, Bugatti's quad-turbo W16 has retired. The Tourbillon that replaces it trades turbochargers for three electric motors — and an all-new naturally aspirated V16 built with Cosworth.

By AutoGlob Staff · July 21, 2026 · 7 min read

Bugatti built the last Chiron in late May 2024. It was the 500th and final example of a car that had defined the brand since its debut at the 2016 Geneva Motor Show, and its production run — spread across the standard Chiron, Sport, Pur Sport, Super Sport and a handful of special editions — closed out what the company calls its W16 era.

That final car wasn't just another Chiron off the line. Built by Bugatti's Sur Mesure bespoke department and named "L'Ultime," it was finished in a graduated paint scheme moving from Atlantic Blue to French Racing Blue — a deliberate callback to the two-tone livery of the original 2016 Geneva show car, with the blue split unevenly across the body rather than along the brand's usual centerline.

What the Chiron actually was

Under the bodywork sat an 8.0-liter quad-turbocharged W16 engine, producing 1,479 horsepower in the base Chiron and up to 1,578 horsepower in the Super Sport variants. Top speed was electronically limited to 261 mph on standard cars and 273 mph on the Super Sport. An earlier, unrestricted Chiron Super Sport 300+ became the first production car verified to exceed 300 mph in a closed-course test in 2019 — a record that still anchors much of the model's reputation.

Bugatti has said every one of the 500 Chiron build slots sold out by January 2022, two years before the final car was completed. First deliveries had begun in March 2017.

The Tourbillon: a hybrid, but not the way you'd expect

Bugatti revealed the Chiron's successor, the Tourbillon, on June 20, 2024, in Molsheim — its first genuinely new model since Croatian manufacturer Rimac took a controlling stake in the company in 2021. Rather than going electric, or keeping the W16, Bugatti and Rimac built something unusual: a brand-new 8.3-liter naturally aspirated V16, developed with engineering partner Cosworth, with no turbochargers at all.

That combustion engine alone makes 1,000 horsepower and revs to 9,000 rpm. It's paired with three electric motors — two on the front axle, one at the rear — drawing from a 24.8 kWh battery, for a combined output of 1,800 horsepower. According to Bugatti's published specifications, the Tourbillon reaches 100 km/h (62 mph) in 2.0 seconds, 300 km/h (186 mph) in under 10 seconds, and tops out at 380 km/h (236 mph) — or 445 km/h (276 mph) with an unlockable "Speed Key." The electric motors alone are rated to give the car roughly 60 km (about 37 miles) of all-electric range.

What actually changes for the person buying one

Bugatti says the Tourbillon shares no components with the Chiron, despite an outwardly similar silhouette. The transmission has moved from between the seats to behind the engine, freeing up cabin space; the instrument cluster is built by Swiss watchmakers from more than 600 components and stays fully analog, a deliberate choice by design director Frank Heyl, who has said oversized touchscreens are what dates a car's interior fastest.

Production is limited to 250 units, priced from roughly €3.8 million (about $4.1 million), with deliveries scheduled to begin in 2026. That's a narrower run than the 500 Chirons or 450 Veyrons that came before it — Bugatti's clearest signal yet that exclusivity, not volume, is the strategy going forward.

The bigger story is what the Tourbillon represents for the hypercar segment as a whole. While most rivals are moving toward electric powertrains to chase acceleration numbers, Bugatti went the other direction: a naturally aspirated engine, built without forced induction, at a moment when regulation is pushing the rest of the industry toward turbos, hybrids, or full battery power. Whether that bet pays off commercially will become clearer once deliveries actually begin.
