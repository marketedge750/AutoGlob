The Instrument Panel as Art: A Study of 1960s Dashboards

Before touchscreens, designers treated the dash like a piece of furniture.

By Elena Cross · July 8, 2026 · 4 min read

Open the door of almost any well-preserved car from the 1960s and the first thing you notice isn't the seats or the wheel — it's the dashboard, laid out less like a control panel and more like a piece of case furniture, with real wood veneer, chrome bezels, and gauges positioned for legibility rather than data density.

Function followed form, on purpose

Designers in this era weren't yet constrained by crash-safety regulations that would later flatten dashboards into padded, injury-minimizing shapes. That freedom let them treat the dash as an extension of the car's exterior styling — sculpted, symmetrical, often asymmetrically accented with a single design flourish that became the model's signature.

The materials tell their own story. Real wood, real metal, and stitched leather were standard even in mid-range cars because plastic molding technology hadn't yet made synthetic materials cheaper and easier to work with. What we now consider a luxury feature was, in the 1960s, simply the only option available.

What got lost in translation

Modern dashboards are unambiguously safer and more functional, packing navigation, climate, and diagnostics into a single screen. But something about the tactile specificity of a manual dial, or a switch you could feel click into place without looking, hasn't been fully replaced — which is part of why restored 1960s interiors still draw such a strong reaction from people who've never known anything but a touchscreen.
