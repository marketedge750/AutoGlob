Naturally Aspirated, By Choice: The Holdouts Building V12s in 2026

A handful of small manufacturers refuse to give up the sound.

By Tom Achebe · July 20, 2026 · 6 min read

Turbocharging and electrification have made naturally aspirated V12 engines almost impossible to justify on paper. They're heavier, less efficient, and more expensive to certify for emissions than practically any alternative. A small number of manufacturers keep building them anyway, and none of them are pretending it's a rational business decision.

The engineering case that isn't really about engineering

A turbocharged V8 can now match or beat a naturally aspirated V12 on every performance metric that shows up in a spec sheet. What it can't replicate is the way a big, unforced-induction engine builds power linearly through the rev range, or the specific harmonic of twelve cylinders firing without a turbo's lag or whoosh interrupting the sound.

Who's still doing it, and why it matters

The manufacturers keeping V12s alive tend to be small, privately controlled, and explicit that the engine is a brand statement rather than a rational engineering choice. They're betting that a segment of buyers will always pay a premium for mechanical drama that a spreadsheet would never recommend — and so far, waiting lists suggest they're right.

Emissions regulations will eventually make this impossible regardless of demand. Most insiders expect the current generation of naturally aspirated V12s to be among the last ever certified for road use, which is part of why the ones already in showrooms are appreciating in value before they've even left the lot.
