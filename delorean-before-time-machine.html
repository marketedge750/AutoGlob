DeLorean Before the Time Machine: The Company That Almost Wasn't

Long before pop culture, there was a factory in Belfast and a very risky bet.

By Tom Achebe · July 12, 2026 · 7 min read

Most people who know the DeLorean know it as a prop — gull-wing doors, stainless steel, a flux capacitor bolted where an engine bay should be. Almost none of them know it was built in Belfast, funded partly by the British government as a jobs program during the height of political violence in Northern Ireland, or that its founder was on trial for cocaine trafficking within two years of the factory opening.

An engineer with a plan and no company

John DeLorean had spent nearly two decades at General Motors before deciding he could build a better sports car than the ones he'd been assigned to manage. He wasn't wrong about the engineering — the car's brushed stainless panels and gull-wing doors were genuinely novel — but he badly misjudged how much it would cost to bring an entirely new manufacturer to market.

The factory that shouldn't have existed

Belfast in 1978 was, by any conventional business logic, the wrong place to build a car factory. The British government backed the project anyway, betting that thousands of manufacturing jobs might do more for the region's stability than another round of policing. For a brief moment, it worked — until quality problems, a stronger dollar, and DeLorean's own legal troubles collapsed the company within three years.

Around 9,000 cars were built before the factory closed for good. Most of what people know about the DeLorean today comes from a movie made after the company had already failed — which means the car's real story, as a genuine and reckless act of industrial ambition, rarely gets told at all.
