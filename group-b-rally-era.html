Group B: The Rally Era That Was Too Fast to Live

Banned after four seasons, its cars are now worth more than houses.

By Marco Reyes · July 10, 2026 · 8 min read

For four seasons in the mid-1980s, rally regulations allowed manufacturers to build cars with almost no limits on power, weight, or aerodynamics, provided they made 200 road-going versions to homologate the design. What resulted were machines with more power-to-weight than contemporary Formula One cars, driven flat-out on public roads lined with spectators standing inches from the racing line.

The physics caught up eventually

Group B cars could accelerate from a standstill to highway speed faster than a driver could process the road ahead, on surfaces with no runoff and crowds that treated the danger as part of the spectacle. It worked, spectacularly, until it didn't.

"We were building cars faster than the sport around them could safely contain."

The end came in one season

A string of fatal accidents in 1986, including one in Portugal that killed several spectators, forced the sport's governing body to ban the category outright for the following season. Manufacturers who had invested heavily in Group B programs were left with cars that suddenly had nowhere to race.

That abrupt ending is exactly why these cars are so valuable today. Production numbers were tiny to begin with, the category's lifespan was cut short before manufacturers could iterate further, and the mythology around the danger has only grown with distance. A well-documented Group B car can now sell for seven figures — not despite the era's recklessness, but because of it.
